Chapter One

Foundations of Software Quality

Every topic in this course rests on what quality means and how it is measured. Master this chapter before all others — it provides the vocabulary for every exam answer you will write.

1.1 What Is Software Quality?

Software quality is deceptively difficult to define because different stakeholders experience the same software differently. An end user cares about ease of use; a developer cares about maintainability; a regulator cares about safety. No single sentence captures all of this — and that is precisely why exam questions ask for multiple perspectives.

Working Definition (for exam use)

Software quality is the totality of features and characteristics of a software product that bear on its ability to satisfy stated and implied needs — encompassing conformance to requirements, fitness for purpose, reliability, usability, maintainability, and the degree to which the development process is controlled and disciplined.

The phrase stated and implied needs is important. Stated needs are documented in requirements. Implied needs are what any reasonable user would expect even if nobody wrote them down — for example, a banking app should never expose passwords even if the specification never said so explicitly.

The Two-Part IEEE Definition

The IEEE definition has two inseparable parts:

  • Conformance to specification. Does the software do what the requirements document says? This is measurable and objective.
  • Fitness for purpose. Even if specifications are met, does the software satisfy what the user actually needed? Software can fully conform to a bad specification and still be low quality.
Exam Alert — Sets 11 & Omega

The question reads "provide what you understand by the term software quality." The word "tebro" in some scanned sets is an OCR error for the word "term." Always answer by combining the IEEE definition with at least two of the five views below.

Chapter Two

Quality Assurance, Quality Control & Defect Management Most repeated topic

This chapter is the single highest-frequency topic cluster in your exam sets. QA vs QC appears in Sets 2, 4, 6, 8, Alpha, Omega, and Set 13. The George Wine case appears verbatim in Sets 2, 3, 4, and 6. Know every concept here with precision.

2.1 Quality Assurance versus Quality Control ×6 sets

Quality Assurance (QA)
  • Process-oriented
  • Proactive — prevents defects
  • Covers the entire SDLC
  • Applies to how work is done
  • Examples: process audits, reviews, standards, training
  • Goal: ensure the right process is followed so defects cannot enter
Quality Control (QC)
  • Product-oriented
  • Reactive — finds defects
  • Applied to specific work products
  • Applies to what was produced
  • Examples: testing, code review, inspection
  • Goal: identify defects in the output so they can be fixed

Why QC is considered part of QA

Even though QC and QA are not the same thing, QC is a subset of QA in the broader sense. QA encompasses all activities that contribute to quality — and QC activities (testing, inspection, reviews) are among the planned, managed activities that QA oversees. QA sets the standards and process; QC executes one specific type of activity within that process to verify the product meets those standards.

One-line exam answer

QA is about preventing defects through process discipline; QC is about finding defects in the product. QC is part of QA because it is one of the planned activities through which QA achieves its goals.

2.2 Software QA Activities (Beyond Testing)

Set 8 and Set Alpha explicitly ask for two SQA activities other than testing. The full list includes:

  • Reviews and inspections — systematic examination of documents, code, and designs by peers or experts to find defects before testing.
  • Audits — formal evaluation of whether the project is following defined processes and standards.
  • Configuration management — controlling changes to software artefacts to prevent unauthorised modifications and track versions.
  • Risk management — identifying and mitigating quality risks before they become defects.
  • Standards compliance — ensuring the project follows organisational and regulatory coding and documentation standards.
  • Training and certification — ensuring all team members have the skills required to perform quality work.

2.3 Defect Prevention ×4 sets

Definition

Defect prevention involves identifying the causes of defects and taking action to stop them from being introduced into the software in the first place. It is entirely proactive — defect prevention happens before development produces a defect, not after.

Two activities for defect prevention:

  • Process improvement and standards enforcement. Defining clear coding standards, documentation requirements, and development processes — and enforcing them — removes the conditions under which defects arise. (This is exactly why George Wine is warned despite his talent: violating coding standards is a defect prevention failure.)
  • Training and skill development. Ensuring developers, testers, and analysts have the right skills to perform their tasks correctly the first time. An undertrained developer introduces more defects regardless of effort.

2.4 Defect Reduction ×4 sets

Definition

Defect reduction involves activities that detect and remove defects that have already been introduced into the software. It is reactive relative to defect prevention but proactive relative to the customer — the goal is to find defects before the user does.

Two activities for defect reduction:

  • Testing (all levels — unit, integration, system). Executing the software with the explicit goal of causing it to fail, thereby revealing defects that can then be fixed.
  • Code and design reviews / inspections. Structured peer examination of code, design documents, and requirements — statistically one of the most cost-effective defect-finding activities in software engineering.

2.5 Defect Containment ×3 sets

Definition

Defect containment involves activities that limit the impact of defects that have escaped into a delivered system. The defect exists and may be known — the goal is to prevent it from causing catastrophic failure or spreading to other parts of the system.

Two activities for defect containment:

  • Fault tolerance and defensive programming. Building the system to continue operating in a degraded but safe state when a component fails, rather than crashing entirely.
  • Patch management and rapid response. Having a process to deliver fixes to users quickly once a defect in production is discovered — minimising the window of exposure.
ConceptTimingFocusRelation to QA vs QC
Defect PreventionBefore developmentThe processPure QA
Defect ReductionDuring developmentThe productQC (within QA)
Defect ContainmentAfter deliveryThe impactOperations & maintenance QA

2.6 The George Wine / Abraham John Case Study ×4 sets — verbatim

This is the exact same question in Sets 2, 3, 4, and 6. The names change (George Wine / George Wiss / Abraham John) but the situation is identical. The answer is always the same: yes, the chief software engineer is correct.

The Scenario

An exceptional programmer produces code with very few errors, meets his schedule, and finds creative solutions — but he consistently ignores documentation requirements and team coding standards. He is warned he will be fired unless he fully complies.

Three reasons to agree with the chief engineer:

1
SQA requires process conformance, not just individual output quality.

Software engineering is a team discipline. SQA activities — reviews, audits, configuration management — all depend on developers producing code and documentation in a standardised, predictable format. George's "original" coding style and missing documentation make peer reviews impossible, break team workflows, and undermine the entire QA process. A brilliant individual who undermines team processes is a net liability.

2
Non-standard documentation creates a maintainability crisis.

Software spends far more time in maintenance than in initial development. When George leaves, changes jobs, or becomes unavailable, his undocumented, non-standard code becomes a black box that no other team member can maintain safely. The cost of this hidden technical debt vastly exceeds the short-term productivity he provides. Maintainability is an ISO 9126 quality characteristic — George's work is measurably low quality by that standard.

3
Defect prevention depends on documentation and standards compliance.

Coding standards and documentation templates exist specifically to prevent defects — they encode accumulated institutional knowledge about common errors, required checks, and safe patterns. By ignoring them, George bypasses these defect prevention mechanisms. Even if his current modules have few errors, the absence of documentation means there is no systematic way to verify this, no audit trail, and no knowledge transfer. The first time someone must modify his code, the defect rate will spike.

Chapter Three

Dependability Metrics & Calculations High-mark chapter

These metrics carry 3–4 marks each and appear in Sets 1, 7, Alpha, Set 12 and Set 13. You must know every formula and be able to execute multi-step calculations cold. The MUELE scenario in Set 1 and the online delivery scenario in Sets 7 and Alpha are nearly identical in structure.

3.1 Availability

Availability measures the proportion of time a system is operational and accessible to users. It is the most commonly cited reliability metric.

Availability Formulas
Availability (A) = MTBF ÷ (MTBF + MTTR)
A = Uptime ÷ (Uptime + Downtime)
Max Downtime = Total Time × (1 − Target Availability)

MTBF = Mean Time Between Failures | MTTR = Mean Time To Repair

3.2 ROCOF — Rate of Occurrence of Failures

ROCOF measures how frequently failures occur over a unit of time. A lower ROCOF indicates a more reliable system.

ROCOF Formulas
ROCOF = Number of failures ÷ Observation period

Units: failures/hour, failures/day, failures per 8-hr shift — match whatever the question specifies

3.3 POFOD — Probability of Failure on Demand

POFOD is the probability that the system will fail when a user makes a request. It is particularly important for safety-critical systems where each request is a potential point of failure.

POFOD Formula
POFOD = Number of failures ÷ Total number of requests

Dimensionless (a probability between 0 and 1)

3.4 MTBF and MTTR

MTBF & MTTR Formulas
MTBF = Total uptime ÷ Number of failures
MTTR = Total downtime ÷ Number of failures

From ROCOF: MTBF = 1 ÷ ROCOF (when ROCOF is in failures/unit time)
Minimum MTBF to satisfy both ROCOF and MTTR: MTBF ≥ (1/ROCOF) − MTTR

3.5 Worked Examples

Worked Example A — MUELE (Set 1)

Given: Target availability 99.5%, POFOD target 2×10⁻¹⁰, ROCOF ≤ 0.02 failures/day, MTTR = 20 hours, 4,000,000 requests, 31-day month.

(a) Maximum total downtime:
Downtime = 31 days × 24 hr/day × (1 − 0.995) = 744 × 0.005 = 3.72 hours

(b) Max failures from POFOD:
Failures = POFOD × Requests = 2×10⁻¹⁰ × 4,000,000 = 0.0008 failures (effectively 0 — extremely stringent)

(c) Max failures from ROCOF:
Failures = ROCOF × Days = 0.02 × 31 = 0.62 failures ≈ at most 0 complete failures per month

(d) Minimum MTBF:
MTBF ≥ (1/0.02) − 20 = 50 − 20 = 30 hours minimum between failures

(e) Achieved availability with 5 failures, MTTR=18 hrs each:
Total downtime = 5 × 18 = 90 hrs | Total time = 744 hrs
A = (744 − 90) ÷ 744 = 654 ÷ 744 = 87.9% — well below the 99.5% target

(f) Why high availability alone does not guarantee good user experience:
A system can be technically available (online) yet slow, confusing, or producing incorrect results. User experience encompasses usability, performance, accuracy, and consistency — none of which availability alone measures.

Worked Example B — Online Delivery Company (Sets 7 & Alpha)

Given: 972 requests, 64 failures (37 crashes × 2 min each, 27 wrong totals), 7 days × 24 hours = 168 hours. Targets: availability ≥ 99%, POFOD < 0.1, ROCOF < 2/8-hr shift.

ROCOF: 64 failures ÷ 168 hours = 0.38 failures/hour = 3.05 failures per 8 hours — FAILS target (<2)

POFOD: 64 ÷ 972 = 0.066 — PASSES target (<0.1)

Availability:
Downtime = 37 crashes × 2 min = 74 min = 1.233 hours
A = (168 − 1.233) ÷ 168 = 166.767 ÷ 168 = 99.27% — PASSES target (≥99%)

Is it ready to ship? No. Although POFOD and availability targets are met, the ROCOF target is failed. 3.05 failures per 8-hour period exceeds the maximum of 2. The system fails too frequently during operation even if each individual failure is short-lived.

Chapter Four

Verification & Validation

V&V underpins all testing and quality assurance activity. The distinction between verification and validation, and the precise meanings of bugs, errors, faults, and failures, appear in multiple sets and carry guaranteed marks.

4.1 V&V — Aims and Distinction

Verification
  • "Are we building the product right?"
  • Checks the product against its specification
  • Can be done without running the software (static)
  • Reviews, inspections, walkthroughs
  • Detects: inconsistencies, omissions, logic errors vs spec
Validation
  • "Are we building the right product?"
  • Checks the product against user needs
  • Requires running the software (dynamic)
  • System testing, user acceptance testing
  • Detects: gaps between spec and actual user need

V&V together aim to establish confidence that the software is both correct with respect to its specification and fit for its intended purpose. The required level of confidence depends on the criticality of the system — a safety-critical hospital system demands near-total confidence; a game tolerates much more residual risk.

Three levels V&V depends on (Set 9)

  • The purpose of the software. Safety-critical systems (medical, aviation) require exhaustive V&V. Non-critical commercial systems accept residual risk.
  • User expectations. Users of novel experimental software accept more bugs than users of mature stable software.
  • Marketing environment. Early-release products in competitive markets may ship with known minor issues to gain market share — a business trade-off on V&V completeness.

4.2 Bugs, Errors, Faults, and Failures ×3 sets

TermDefinitionExample
ErrorA human mistake — a developer's incorrect understanding, decision, or action during design or coding.A developer misunderstands the requirement and writes subtraction instead of addition.
Bug / Defect / FaultThe manifestation of an error in the code — a flaw in the software artefact that, under certain conditions, will cause the system to behave incorrectly. "Bug" and "defect" are informal terms for "fault."The line of code that performs subtraction instead of addition sitting in the source file.
FailureAn observable deviation from expected behaviour when the software executes — what the user actually experiences when a fault is triggered.The user's bank account is debited instead of credited. The fault fired, causing a failure.
Key distinction — Fault vs Failure

A fault can exist in code for years without causing a failure — it only becomes a failure when the specific conditions that trigger it occur during execution. This is why testing cannot guarantee the absence of faults: you can only test the conditions you think to test.

Defect vs Bug (Set 9 / Set Alpha)

A defect is the formal, process-management term — it covers any deviation from requirements detected at any stage of the SDLC (in a requirements document, a design, or code). A bug is the informal term typically used by developers to mean a coding-level fault. All bugs are defects, but not all defects are bugs — a defect in a requirements document is not a bug.

4.3 Fuzzing and Taint Analysis (Set 1)

Fuzzing

Definition

Fuzzing is a dynamic verification technique that feeds a program randomly generated, malformed, or unexpected inputs at high volume and monitors for crashes, hangs, or assertion violations. The goal is to find inputs the developer never anticipated — particularly useful for finding security vulnerabilities and input validation failures.

Taint Analysis

Definition

Taint analysis is a dynamic verification technique that tracks the flow of untrusted ("tainted") data through a program from its entry point (user input, network data) to any point where it could cause harm (SQL query, file write, system call). If tainted data reaches a sensitive sink without being sanitised, a vulnerability is flagged.

4.4 Formal Verification versus Testing (Set 8 / Set Alpha)

Chapter Five

Software Testing Fundamentals

The theoretical foundations of testing — definitions, principles, white vs black box, and test case structure. White box and black box testing appear in Sets 8, 9, and Alpha.

5.1 Testing Definition (ANSI/IEEE 1059)

ANSI/IEEE 1059 Definition

Software testing is the process of operating a system or component under specified conditions, observing or recording the results, and making an evaluation of some aspect of the system or component.

Those who perform software testing include: developers (unit testing), dedicated testers/QA engineers, test automation engineers, users (acceptance testing), and independent test teams.

5.2 Why Testing Cannot Prove the Absence of Bugs

This is a fundamental theoretical point attributed to Edsger Dijkstra:

  • The input space is infinite (or astronomically large). Even a simple function accepting a 32-bit integer has over 4 billion possible inputs. Testing all of them is impossible in practice.
  • State space explosion. Real programs have internal state that multiplies possible execution paths exponentially. The number of unique paths through a non-trivial program exceeds the number of atoms in the universe.
  • Testing proves execution, not absence. A test that passes proves only that the program behaved correctly for that specific input under those specific conditions. A fault may exist for inputs you never tested.
The quote to remember

"Program testing can be used to show the presence of bugs, but never to show their absence." — Edsger W. Dijkstra

5.3 White Box Testing ×4 sets

What you verify in white box testing

White box testing verifies the internal structure, logic, and implementation of the software. The tester has full visibility of the source code and designs test cases to exercise specific paths, branches, conditions, and statements within the code.

What white box testing covers:

  • Statement coverage — every line of code is executed at least once.
  • Branch coverage — every decision (if/else, switch) takes each possible branch at least once.
  • Path coverage — every possible execution path through the code is traversed.
  • Condition coverage — every boolean sub-expression evaluates to both true and false.

White box testing is performed by developers and is most effective at the unit and integration test levels. It is the basis for cyclomatic complexity and control flow graph analysis.

5.4 Black Box Testing ×4 sets

What you verify in black box testing

Black box testing verifies the software's external behaviour against its specification, with no knowledge of internal implementation. The tester treats the system as a black box — inputs go in, outputs come out, and the tester checks whether outputs match expectations.

Four categories of errors uncovered by black box testing (Set 11/Omega):

  • Incorrect or missing functions — the software does not implement a required behaviour at all.
  • Interface errors — the system does not correctly accept inputs or produce outputs in the expected format.
  • Errors in data structures or external database access — data is stored, retrieved, or manipulated incorrectly.
  • Behaviour or performance errors — the system is too slow, non-deterministic, or behaves differently under load than under normal conditions.

5.5 Test Case Components (Set 1)

A well-designed test case contains three main components:

Chapter Six

Test Design Techniques High-mark chapter

Equivalence partitioning, boundary value analysis, and cyclomatic complexity carry 6–14 marks per question and appear in at least 4 sets each. The Tax System and Armstrong Number questions are reproduced verbatim across Sets 7 and Alpha.

6.1 Equivalence Partitioning ×4 sets

Definition

Equivalence partitioning divides the input domain into classes of data where all values in a class are expected to be processed identically by the software. One test case per class is sufficient — testing multiple values in the same class adds no additional coverage.

How to identify partitions:

  • Valid partitions — input ranges the software should accept and process correctly.
  • Invalid partitions — input ranges the software should reject with an appropriate error message.
Tax System Worked Example (Sets 7 & Alpha — verbatim)

Specification: For a person with salary < 20,000 AND who is married, tax is recalculated. If they also have >2 AND <5 children, an additional 10% reduction applies.

Input variables and classes:

Salary: Valid: salary < 20,000 | Invalid: salary ≥ 20,000

Marital status: Valid: married | Invalid: not married

Number of children:
— Class 1 (invalid lower): children ≤ 2 (no additional reduction)
— Class 2 (valid): children = 3 or 4 (10% additional reduction applies)
— Class 3 (invalid upper): children ≥ 5 (no additional reduction)

Most effective class for testing children: Class 2 (3 ≤ children ≤ 4) — this is the only class that triggers the 10% reduction rule, making it the most critical to verify correctly. A single test with children = 3 or children = 4 covers this entire class.

6.2 Boundary Value Analysis ×3 sets

Definition

Boundary value analysis extends equivalence partitioning by testing at and immediately adjacent to the boundaries between partitions. Faults cluster at boundaries because developers tend to use < when they should use ≤, or start loops at 1 instead of 0.

For each boundary, test these values:

  • The value just below the boundary (boundary − 1)
  • The value at the boundary exactly
  • The value just above the boundary (boundary + 1)
Airplane Seat BVA (Sets 13 & Omega)

Inputs: Ticket (1001–1150), Row (1–25), Seat (1–6)

Valid partitions: Ticket ∈ [1001,1150], Row ∈ [1,25], Seat ∈ [1,6]

Invalid partitions: Ticket <1001 or >1150, Row <1 or >25, Seat <1 or >6

EP vs BVA — key difference

Equivalence partitioning selects one representative value from the middle of each class. Boundary value analysis focuses specifically on the edges between classes, where off-by-one errors occur. BVA supplements EP — it does not replace it.

6.3 Cyclomatic Complexity ×2 sets

Definition

Cyclomatic complexity (V(G)) is a quantitative measure of the number of linearly independent paths through a program's source code. It provides a lower bound on the number of test cases required for complete branch coverage.

Three methods to calculate V(G) — all give the same answer:

Cyclomatic Complexity Formulas
Method 1 (Graph): V(G) = E − N + 2
E = number of edges (arrows) | N = number of nodes (boxes)

Method 2 (Predicates): V(G) = P + 1
P = number of predicate nodes (decision points: if, while, for, case)

Method 3 (Regions): V(G) = number of enclosed regions in the graph + 1
Count closed areas formed by the graph edges
Armstrong Number — Cyclomatic Complexity (Sets 7 & Alpha)

The Armstrong number code has these predicate nodes: (1) the while loop condition, (2) the if condition. So P = 2.

V(G) = P + 1 = 2 + 1 = 3

This means there are 3 linearly independent paths and you need a minimum of 3 test cases for full branch coverage.

Basis paths:
Path 1: while condition false immediately (num = 0 — edge case)
Path 2: while executes, sum ≠ num (not Armstrong — e.g., 123)
Path 3: while executes, sum = num (is Armstrong — e.g., 153)

Test cases:
TC1: input = 0 → "0 is not an Armstrong number" (tests false while entry)
TC2: input = 123 → "123 is not an Armstrong number" (1³+2³+3³ = 36 ≠ 123)
TC3: input = 153 → "153 is an Armstrong number" (1³+5³+3³ = 153)

Chapter Seven

Software Metrics ×5 sets for FP calculation

Function Points is the single most repeatedly tested calculation in your entire exam set — the same numbers (55 inputs, 35 outputs, 40 inquiries, 8 files, 5 interfaces) appear in Sets 9, 13, Omega, Set 12, and Set Alpha. Know the weights table and the procedure cold.

7.1 Function Points — Theory

Function Points (FP) measure software size based on the functionality delivered to the user, independent of programming language or implementation technology. They answer: "how much does this software do?" without asking "how is it done?"

The five function types:

TypeAbbrev.DescriptionWeights (Low/Avg/High)
External InputEIUser inputs that add, change, or delete data (forms, transactions)3 / 4 / 6
External OutputEOOutputs that provide data to the user (reports, screens, messages)4 / 5 / 7
External InquiryEQInput/output pairs — user queries a result without changing data3 / 4 / 6
Internal Logical FileILFLogical groups of data maintained inside the system (master files, tables)7 / 10 / 15
External Interface FileEIFData referenced from another system (external databases, files)5 / 7 / 10

Why use FP over LOC?

  • Language independence. 100 lines of Java does not equal 100 lines of Python for the same functionality. FP measures what the software does, not how it does it.
  • Early estimation. FP can be estimated from requirements before any code is written. LOC can only be counted after coding is complete.
  • User perspective. FP measures delivered functionality, which aligns with what the customer is paying for — not implementation effort.

7.3 The FFP Metric (Sets 11, 12, Omega)

Definition

FFP (Files, Flows, Processes) is a simplified sizing metric that estimates software size from three architectural counts: the number of Files (data stores), Flows (data movements), and Processes (transformations).

FFP Formulas
Size (FFP) = Files + Flows + Processes
Cost Estimate = Constant × FFP Size

The constant represents the organisation's cost per FFP unit (provided in the question)
Worked Example — M.Tech developers (Sets 11 & Omega)

Files = 10, Flows = 56, Processes = 101, Constant = 1031

Size = 10 + 56 + 101 = 167 FFP

Cost = 1031 × 167 = UGX/$ 172,177

Worked Example — DELEAH Software (Set 12)

Files = 12, Flows = 48, Processes = 81, Constant = 1021

Size = 12 + 48 + 81 = 141 FFP

Cost = 1021 × 141 = 143,961

7.4 Halstead Complexity Metrics (Set 12)

Halstead metrics measure software complexity by counting operators and operands in source code. In the exam, you are given a Pascal or C program and asked to populate a table.

Halstead Formulas
n1 = number of distinct operators (unique)
n2 = number of distinct operands (unique)
N1 = total operator occurrences
N2 = total operand occurrences

Program Length N = N1 + N2
Vocabulary n = n1 + n2
Volume V = N × log₂(n)
Difficulty D = (n1/2) × (N2/n2)
Effort E = D × V

Identifying operators vs operands:

  • Operators: Keywords (BEGIN, END, WHILE, IF, READ, WRITE), arithmetic symbols (+, −, ×, ÷), assignment (:=), punctuation that acts functionally (brackets in expressions)
  • Operands: Variable names (Sum, Prod, a, b, c), literal values (numbers, strings)
Chapter Eight

Software Configuration Management ×4 sets

SCM appears as a major question in Sets 7, 8, 9, 13, Alpha, and Omega. You need the definition, the five primary activities, configuration items, SCMP contents, and the distinction between version, revision, and release.

8.1 SCM — Definition & Benefits

Definition

Software Configuration Management (SCM) is the discipline of tracking and controlling changes to software and its related artefacts throughout the software development lifecycle — ensuring that the right version of the right item is available to the right people at the right time.

Six benefits of SCM:

  • Version control — maintains a complete history of all changes, allowing rollback to any prior state.
  • Change management — ensures all changes are properly reviewed, approved, and tracked before implementation.
  • Traceability — links every change to the requirement, defect, or decision that caused it.
  • Concurrent development support — allows multiple developers to work on the same codebase simultaneously without overwriting each other's work.
  • Release management — ensures only tested, approved configurations are released to production.
  • Audit trail — provides a complete record for compliance, debugging, and organisational knowledge retention.

8.2 Five Primary SCM Activities

#ActivityWhat it involves
1Configuration IdentificationIdentifying all items that will be placed under configuration control (code, requirements, designs, test plans, user manuals), naming them uniquely, and establishing their baseline versions.
2Configuration Control / Change ManagementEstablishing a formal process for requesting, evaluating, approving, and implementing changes to controlled items. Prevents unauthorised changes from entering the system.
3Configuration Status AccountingRecording and reporting the current status of all configuration items — what version each is at, what changes are pending, what has been approved and when.
4Configuration AuditingVerifying that configuration items conform to their specifications and that the SCM process is being followed. Two types: functional audit (does it work as specified?) and physical audit (is the build complete and correct?).
5Release and Delivery ManagementControlling the packaging, storage, and delivery of approved software releases to internal or external recipients — ensuring what is delivered matches what was tested and approved.

8.3 Configuration Items

A configuration item (CI) is any artefact placed under formal SCM control. For a results management system project, four CIs would include:

  • Source code files — all .java, .py, .sql or other code files that implement the system.
  • Requirements specification document — the formal document defining what the system must do.
  • Test plans and test cases — documented test procedures and expected results.
  • Database schema and migration scripts — the definitions of all tables, relationships, and stored procedures.
  • (Others: design documents, user manuals, deployment configuration files, build scripts)

8.4 SCMP — Software Configuration Management Plan Contents

The SCMP defines how SCM will be conducted on a specific project. Four contents it must include:

  • Introduction and scope — the project covered, the purpose of the plan, applicable standards and references.
  • SCM activities — detailed description of how each activity (identification, control, status accounting, auditing, release) will be performed on this project.
  • Roles and responsibilities — who the SCM manager is, what the CCB (Change Control Board) membership is, and what each participant's responsibilities are.
  • Tools, techniques, and methods — which version control system, issue tracker, and build tools will be used and how they are configured.
  • (Others: milestone schedule, training requirements, applicable policies)

8.6 Version, Revision, and Release

TermDefinitionExample
VersionA distinct instance of a configuration item at a specific point in time. Every change that is committed creates a new version. Versions form a history.requirements_v1.docx, requirements_v2.docx — two different states of the same document.
RevisionA change to an existing version that corrects or improves it without changing its fundamental purpose or identity. A revision typically increments a minor version number.v1.0 → v1.1 → v1.2 are revisions of version 1. A bug fix patch is a revision.
ReleaseA version (or set of versions) that has been formally approved, tested, and made available to users (internal or external). Not every version becomes a release — only those that pass all quality gates.v2.0 is released to customers after passing all acceptance tests. v1.9-beta was a version but not a release.
Version vs Revision — exam distinction

A version is any snapshot of an item. A revision is a minor corrective change within the same major version. Think: v1.0 → v1.1 is a revision; v1.x → v2.0 is a new version. All revisions produce new versions, but not all new versions are revisions.

Baseline (Sets 9, 11, Alpha)

Definition

A baseline is a formally reviewed and agreed-upon specification or product that serves as the basis for further development and can only be changed through formal change control procedures. Once a baseline is set, nothing in it can change without CCB approval.

9.1 Stakeholders — Definition and Types

Definition

A stakeholder is any individual, group, or organisation that has an interest in, is affected by, or can affect the development, deployment, or operation of a software system. Stakeholders may have conflicting interests and priorities.

9.2 ACMIS Student Role and Expectations (Sets 2, 4, 6)

Role: End User / Consumer

As a student, you are an end user of ACMIS. You interact with it to access academic services — you do not modify the system, administer it, or develop it.

Three expectations in that role:

  • Correct and accurate results. Course registration, examination results, and timetable information must be accurate. An incorrect result display or wrong course registration has direct academic consequences. (ISO 9126: Functionality — Accuracy)
  • Availability and responsiveness. The system must be available during critical periods (registration deadlines, results release) and respond quickly even under high concurrent load from thousands of students. A system that is down on results day is a quality failure regardless of its technical correctness. (ISO 9126: Reliability — Availability, Efficiency — Time behaviour)
  • Usability and clarity. Navigation must be intuitive enough that a student with no technical training can complete registration, view results, and access documents without assistance. Confusing interfaces generate errors and frustration. (ISO 9126: Usability)

Process that ensures expectations are met: User Acceptance Testing (UAT)

UAT involves actual students testing the system under realistic conditions against their real-world tasks before the system goes live. Only when representative users confirm the system meets their needs is it accepted. UAT is the validation activity that bridges the gap between what developers built and what students actually need.

9.4 Why Quality Is Engineered, Not Inspected In

The HR software project case (Sets 13 & Omega) asks you to agree that "quality is engineered as part of the project." The key insight is:

  • Quality cannot be added after the fact. A poorly designed system cannot be made high-quality by testing more at the end. Testing can find and remove defects, but it cannot add the reliability, maintainability, or usability that was never designed in.
  • Training and skill development before the project starts is a defect prevention activity — the project case explicitly organises training for team members lacking skills. This is QA engineering quality into the team before code is written.
  • Reviews and expert opinions on documentation are verification activities embedded into the workflow — not added at the end — ensuring each artefact meets standards before being used as input to the next phase.
  • The configuration management database is an SCM activity that protects quality by controlling access to, and changes of, all project artefacts throughout development.
Two reasons the project needs QC/QA

1. Complexity and team size — with multiple developers, reviewers, and approvers working concurrently, formal QA processes prevent inconsistency, miscommunication, and conflicting changes from degrading the product.
2. Documentation volume and criticality — a human resource management system handles sensitive personal data; any defect in data handling, access control, or reporting can have serious legal and organisational consequences. QC activities (reviews, testing) are required to ensure correctness before deployment.
